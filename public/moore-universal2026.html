/ Healthcare · Brand Strategy

Folks Medical — When trust is the product, the brand has to earn it first.

Folks Medical wasn't trying to look better. They were trying to be understood differently — by healthcare professionals, patients, and partners all at once.

01 / The Situation

When Folks Medical came to us, they weren't trying to 'look better.' They were trying to fix something deeper — how people understand their role in healthcare. They operate in a space where trust is everything. If people don't understand you, they don't trust you. And if they don't trust you, they don't engage.

02 / What Was Happening

The challenge wasn't design. It was perception. The organisation had been doing good work. But different audiences — healthcare professionals, everyday patients, institutional partners — were all forming different understandings of what Folks Medical actually was. And those understandings weren't always accurate. That inconsistency was creating friction everywhere. In conversations. In partnerships. In the way people decided whether to recommend them or return.

03 / What We Discovered

We realised that the visual identity wasn't the problem — it was a symptom. The underlying issue was that no one, internally or externally, had a single clear answer to a simple question: What does Folks Medical actually stand for, and who is it primarily for? Until that question was answered, no amount of design work would stick.

04 / What We Did

We worked from the inside out — starting with clarity around what they actually stand for, how they should be understood, and what needed to shift before anything visual was introduced. Every decision was anchored on one question: How do we make this make sense to both a healthcare professional and an everyday patient? We developed a brand architecture that could hold both audiences without speaking to neither — a unified voice that felt credible in a clinical context and accessible in a human one.

05 / What Changed

What came out wasn't just a brand system. It was a clearer way of communicating value. The organisation now had language that could travel — across contexts, across audiences, across channels. Staff could explain what they do in one sentence. Partners understood the offering without a presentation. And patients felt something they hadn't before: that this place knew who they were talking to.

/ Resources · Identity Design

Bacceferous — Competence doesn't need noise. It needs clarity.

Operating in oil and gas licensing, Bacceferous faced a familiar challenge in complex industries: how do you present something technically sophisticated without losing the authority that makes it credible?

01 / The Situation

Bacceferous came with a very specific challenge — how do you present a technical, highly regulated business in a way that still feels credible, structured, and forward-facing? They operate in the oil and gas licensing space, where complexity is normal, but clarity is rare.

02 / What Was Happening

Most companies in this sector either overcomplicate their communication or strip it down so much that it loses authority. Bacceferous had fallen into a middle ground that satisfied neither: technically accurate, but visually and verbally inconsistent. The brand didn't reflect the rigour of the organisation behind it.

03 / What We Discovered

In highly regulated industries, the first thing people look for isn't excitement — it's confidence. They want to feel that you have done this before, that you know the rules, and that you won't create problems for them. The brand wasn't failing to communicate enthusiasm. It was failing to communicate competence.

04 / What We Did

We had to find the balance — between technical precision and accessible communication. Our work focused on building a brand structure that communicates confidence without noise: something that feels precise, controlled, and intentional. Every visual element was a deliberate choice. Nothing decorative. Nothing that existed for its own sake. The identity was built to signal one thing above everything else: this organisation knows exactly what it's doing.

05 / What Changed

The result was a brand identity that doesn't try to impress. It simply communicates competence. And in that industry, that's often enough to win trust. Bacceferous now presents consistently and confidently across all touchpoints — from pitch documents to digital presence — with a clarity that matches the seriousness of what they do.

/ Mining · Brand Identity

AV Mines — Shifting perception in an industry built on extraction.

Mining is often perceived as distant, extractive, and disconnected from the communities around it. AV Mines needed their brand to tell a different story — without pretending to be something they weren't.

01 / The Situation

AV Mines operates in a space where perception is everything. Mining is often seen as extractive, distant, and disconnected from people. Their goal was to shift that perception — towards responsibility, structure, and long-term thinking. The challenge was not just visual identity, but emotional positioning.

02 / What Was Happening

The industry carries weight that follows every brand operating within it. Regardless of how responsibly a company operates, the visual and verbal signals of the brand either reinforce or challenge that perception. AV Mines was doing the work — operating with structure and long-term intent. But the brand wasn't communicating that. It looked like every other mining company: hard, operational, impersonal.

03 / What We Discovered

The brand needed to reframe what AV Mines actually is. Not just a mining company, but a structured, value-driven resource organisation with genuine long-term thinking. The shift wasn't about making them look softer. It was about making them look more intentional — because intentionality is what separates extractive operations from responsible ones.

04 / What We Did

We worked on reframing how the brand is perceived — from a purely operational mining company to a structured, value-driven resource organisation with long-term intent. The identity system was built to feel grounded. Strong. Uncomplicated. Nothing decorative for the sake of attention. Because in industries like this, excess communication often creates doubt — not clarity.

05 / What Changed

AV Mines now presents with the kind of quiet confidence that their actual operations deserve. The brand communicates responsibility without announcing it. It signals long-term thinking without promising what can't be guaranteed. It looks like the kind of organisation partners and communities can take seriously — because it is.

/ Technology · Brand Identity

VaultGenic — Making digital infrastructure feel trustworthy, not technical.

VaultGenic exists in the digital infrastructure space — where everything is technical, but very little is understood by the people who actually need to make decisions about it.

01 / The Situation

VaultGenic exists in the digital infrastructure space — where everything is technical, but very little is understood by the people who actually need it. Their challenge was simple to say, but hard to solve: How do you make something complex feel understandable without making it feel basic?

02 / What Was Happening

The people making decisions about digital infrastructure are often not the people building it. They're executives, operations leads, and risk managers who need to trust what they're buying without necessarily understanding every component. VaultGenic's communications were aimed at the engineers. But the decisions were being made by the non-engineers. That mismatch was costing them opportunities.

03 / What We Discovered

The problem wasn't technical communication — it was emotional positioning. The question wasn't 'how do we explain what we do?' The question was: 'what does someone need to feel to trust us with something this important?' The answer was: stability, security, and reliability. Not features. Not specs. Not technical superiority.

04 / What We Did

We approached it by focusing less on technical explanation and more on perception structure. Instead of trying to explain everything, we helped them communicate what matters most — stability, security, and trust. The brand system reflects that thinking: controlled, minimal, and precise. Nothing loud. Nothing distracting. Just a quiet sense of reliability that communicates competence to decision-makers before a single spec sheet is opened.

05 / What Changed

VaultGenic now speaks to the right audience in the right language. The brand feels like what it is: a serious infrastructure provider for serious organisations. Decision-makers can look at it and immediately understand what it stands for — not because it explains everything, but because it signals the right things. That's what good brand strategy does. It makes trust possible before the conversation even starts.

/ Healthcare · Brand System

Healingways — The brand that feels like the care it provides.

Healthcare brands walk a difficult line between clinical credibility and human warmth. Healingways needed to be both — trusted by professionals, felt by patients.

01 / The Situation

Healingways sits in a deeply human space — health, recovery, and wellbeing. The challenge here wasn't complexity. It was emotion. Healthcare brands often swing between two extremes: too clinical or too emotional. Healingways needed to sit in the middle — professional enough to be trusted, human enough to be felt.

02 / What Was Happening

The existing brand felt sterile. It was competent-looking but cold — the kind of identity that signals professionalism without communicating care. In a health and wellbeing context, that coldness becomes a barrier. People seeking care aren't just looking for competence. They're looking for safety — psychological safety as much as physical.

03 / What We Discovered

The brand needed to communicate 'I understand what you're going through' and 'I know how to help' at the same time. Those are two very different messages, but they need to come from one consistent voice. Most brands in this space prioritise one and neglect the other. The real work was finding the voice that could hold both.

04 / What We Did

We focused on tone first. Then structure. Then identity. The goal was to create a brand that feels safe without feeling distant. Something people can look at and quietly think: 'I understand what this place stands for.' Because in healthcare, clarity is comfort. When people feel uncertain, unclear, or overwhelmed, a brand that communicates with calm confidence becomes part of the care itself.

05 / What Changed

Healingways now has a brand that does what a good healthcare provider does — it makes people feel seen before they've spoken. The identity balances warmth and professionalism with enough intention that neither quality cancels out the other. Something that felt right about the final system: patients didn't describe it as 'nice' or 'professional.' They described it as 'calm.' That was exactly what we were aiming for.

We design identity systems that are built to endure — not trend-chasing exercises, but deliberate visual languages rooted in strategy.

Why We Exist

The problem we kept seeing.

Franckmoore started Moore Universal after observing the same problem across different sectors: organisations doing genuinely meaningful work that people didn't fully understand.

That gap is expensive. It costs opportunities, partnerships, funding, and trust. And it's almost always fixable — if you approach it the right way.

Our Philosophy

Strategy before everything else.

We prioritise clarity over cleverness. We prioritise honesty over impressiveness. And we'd rather help you solve the right problem than deliver beautiful work that doesn't change anything.
